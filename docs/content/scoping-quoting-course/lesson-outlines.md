# Lesson Outlines — Scoping & Quoting for Restoration Professionals

> GP-131 | 8 modules | 32 lessons
> Each outline provides learning objectives, key concepts, standards references and a practical takeaway.

---

## Module 1: Introduction to Scoping

### Lesson 1.1 — What is a Scope of Works? (Video, 12 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Define a scope of works and explain its role in the restoration workflow
- Distinguish between a scope, an estimate and a quote
- Identify the key components of a complete scope document

**Key Concepts:** A scope of works is the foundational document that defines every aspect of a restoration project — what happened, what is affected, what must be done, and what resources are required. It bridges the gap between the initial site assessment and the priced estimate delivered to the client or insurer. A robust scope prevents disputes, reduces rework and ensures all parties share a common understanding of the project boundaries. Students will examine real-world examples of well-structured scopes contrasted with incomplete ones, highlighting how missing detail leads to scope creep and margin erosion.

**Standards:** IICRC S500 Section 2 (Principles of Water Damage Restoration)

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will be able to list the eight essential sections of a scope document from memory.

---

### Lesson 1.2 — The Role of the Estimator (Video, 10 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Describe the estimator's responsibilities on a restoration project
- Explain how the estimator interacts with technicians, project managers, insurers and clients
- Recognise the skills and certifications valued for estimators in Australia

**Key Concepts:** The estimator is the commercial bridge between technical restoration work and the financial outcome of a project. This lesson covers the day-to-day workflow of a restoration estimator — from receiving a job allocation, conducting the site inspection, writing the scope, pricing the estimate, and managing supplements. Students explore how the estimator role differs in small firms (where one person may scope, price and project-manage) versus large organisations with dedicated estimating teams. IICRC WRT certification and experience with Xactimate or SimPRO are highlighted as industry expectations.

**Standards:** IICRC WRT competency framework

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will map the estimator's touchpoints across a typical insurance claim timeline.

---

### Lesson 1.3 — Industry Standards: IICRC S500 & AS/NZS (Text, 15 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Identify the key IICRC and Australian/New Zealand standards relevant to restoration scoping
- Explain how IICRC S500 and S520 guide scoping decisions
- Understand the role of AS/NZS 4349.1 in building inspections

**Key Concepts:** Restoration scoping does not occur in a vacuum — it is governed by industry standards that define best practice, protect the restorer legally, and provide a common language for all stakeholders. This lesson introduces IICRC S500 (water damage), S520 (mould remediation), and their relationship to Australian standards including AS/NZS 4349.1 (building inspections). Students learn how to reference these standards in scope documents to strengthen their professional position, particularly when negotiating with loss adjusters or defending scope line items. The lesson also covers WHS obligations under the model WHS Act as adopted by Australian states and territories.

**Standards:** IICRC S500, IICRC S520, AS/NZS 4349.1, Model WHS Act 2011

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will create a quick-reference card of the five most-cited standard clauses used in water damage scopes.

## Module 2: Site Assessment & Documentation

### Lesson 2.1 — Initial Site Inspection Checklist (Video, 18 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Follow a structured site inspection protocol from arrival to departure
- Identify and document WHS hazards including asbestos, electrical and structural risks
- Record source identification, affected areas, and preliminary category/class assessment

**Key Concepts:** A systematic site inspection is the foundation of every accurate scope. This lesson walks through a step-by-step inspection protocol: safety check on arrival (electrical isolation, slip hazards, ACM identification), source identification and isolation confirmation, room-by-room walkthrough, preliminary moisture readings, and stakeholder communication. Students learn to use a standardised checklist that ensures nothing is missed under time pressure. The lesson emphasises that thoroughness at the inspection stage directly correlates with fewer supplements and disputes later in the project.

**Standards:** IICRC S500 Section 5, Model WHS Act 2011, Safe Work Australia codes of practice

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will receive a downloadable site inspection checklist template ready for field use.

---

### Lesson 2.2 — Photography & Evidence Documentation (Video, 14 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Apply a systematic photographic documentation method for restoration sites
- Capture establishing, mid-range and detail shots for each affected area
- Organise and label evidence files for retrieval during claims and disputes

**Key Concepts:** Photographs are the restorer's most powerful evidence tool. This lesson covers the three-tier photography method: wide establishing shots (context), mid-range area shots (scope of damage), and close-up detail shots (specific damage or readings). Students learn file naming conventions, metadata requirements, and folder structures that support rapid retrieval months after the job. The lesson also addresses video documentation for complex losses and the use of 360-degree cameras. Best practice for timestamping and chain-of-custody when images may be used in legal proceedings is introduced.

**Standards:** IICRC S500 Section 5 (Documentation), AS/NZS 4349.1

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will photograph a simulated damage scenario using the three-tier method and organise the images into a compliant folder structure.

---

### Lesson 2.3 — Moisture Mapping Fundamentals (Video, 20 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Explain the purpose and methodology of moisture mapping in restoration
- Select the appropriate moisture meter (pin vs pinless) for different materials
- Create an accurate moisture map using a grid pattern with dry standard comparison

**Key Concepts:** Moisture mapping is the scientific backbone of water damage scoping. This lesson covers the principles of moisture measurement, the differences between pin-type (resistance) and pinless (capacitance/RF) meters, and when each is appropriate. Students learn to establish a dry standard from unaffected materials, take systematic grid readings, and plot results on a floor plan. The lesson explains how moisture maps drive scope decisions — determining which materials require drying, which require removal, and where the boundary of the affected area lies. Thermal imaging is introduced as a complementary tool for identifying hidden moisture.

**Standards:** IICRC S500 Section 9 (Monitoring and Evaluation)

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will produce a moisture map from provided meter readings and floor plan, identifying the drying boundary.

## Module 3: Water Damage Scoping

### Lesson 3.1 — Category & Class of Water Damage (Video, 16 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Classify water damage by category (1, 2, 3) based on contamination level
- Classify water damage by class (1, 2, 3, 4) based on the rate of evaporation and material absorption
- Explain how category and class determine the scoping approach

**Key Concepts:** The IICRC S500 classification system is the restorer's primary framework for scoping water damage. Category defines the contamination level: Category 1 (clean source), Category 2 (grey water — significant contamination), Category 3 (grossly unsanitary — sewage, floodwater). Class defines the extent of absorption: Class 1 (minimal — small area, low-porosity materials) through Class 4 (specialty drying — materials with very low permeance such as hardwood or concrete). Students learn that category can escalate over time if water is not addressed promptly, and that class determines equipment requirements. The combined category/class assessment directly informs every line item in the scope.

**Standards:** IICRC S500 Sections 3 and 4

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will classify five real-world loss scenarios by category and class from provided descriptions and photographs.

---

### Lesson 3.2 — Affected Materials Inventory (Video, 14 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Conduct a systematic inventory of all affected building materials and contents
- Determine restorability based on material type, category of water and duration of exposure
- Document quantities for accurate scope line items

**Key Concepts:** An affected materials inventory is a room-by-room, material-by-material record of everything within the damage boundary. This lesson teaches students to categorise materials as restorable (dry in place), non-restorable (remove and replace), or requiring specialist assessment. Decisions are driven by the material's porosity, the category of water, and the duration of exposure. Students learn to measure and record quantities — linear metres of skirting, square metres of carpet, number of plasterboard sheets — that translate directly into scope line items. The lesson highlights common errors including underestimating affected area and failing to account for concealed materials.

**Standards:** IICRC S500 Section 7 (Building Materials and Systems)

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will complete an affected materials inventory template for a simulated two-room water loss.

---

### Lesson 3.3 — Drying Equipment Selection & Placement (Video, 18 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Select appropriate drying equipment based on class of water damage and room conditions
- Calculate the required number of air movers and dehumidifiers using industry ratios
- Position equipment for optimal airflow and drying efficiency

**Key Concepts:** Equipment selection and placement is where the scope translates into operational reality. This lesson covers dehumidifier types (refrigerant vs desiccant) and their suitability for different conditions (temperature, humidity, material type). Students learn the standard 4:1 air mover to dehumidifier ratio as a starting point, then adjust for room geometry, material loading and environmental conditions. Placement principles include creating a closed drying circuit, positioning air movers for laminar or focused airflow, and placing dehumidifiers to process the most humid air. The lesson emphasises that the scope must accurately reflect the equipment required — over-specifying increases costs while under-specifying extends drying time and risk.

**Standards:** IICRC S500 Section 8 (Drying Procedures)

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will create a drying equipment plan for a Class 2 loss in a 3-bedroom house, including equipment count and placement diagram.

---

### Lesson 3.4 — Scope Writing: Water Jobs (Text, 25 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Write a complete scope of works for a water damage restoration job
- Structure line items for extraction, demolition, drying, treatment and reinstatement
- Cross-reference scope items to moisture map data and photographic evidence

**Key Concepts:** This lesson brings together all previous water damage content into a practical scope-writing exercise. Students work through a realistic loss scenario from initial data (moisture map, photos, materials inventory, category/class assessment) and produce a structured scope document. The scope follows a logical sequence: emergency response (extraction, electrical isolation), demolition (non-restorable materials), treatment (antimicrobial, containment), drying (equipment specification, monitoring schedule), and reinstatement (replacement materials, finishes). Each line item is cross-referenced to supporting evidence. Students learn to write clear, defensible scope notes that withstand scrutiny from loss adjusters.

**Standards:** IICRC S500, AS/NZS 4349.1

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will produce a complete water damage scope document for a simulated residential loss.

## Module 4: Mould & Contamination Scoping

### Lesson 4.1 — Mould Assessment Protocol (Video, 20 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Describe the standard mould assessment protocol per IICRC S520
- Identify visible and hidden mould indicators during a site inspection
- Determine when third-party environmental testing is required

**Key Concepts:** Mould scoping begins with a thorough assessment that goes beyond visible growth. This lesson covers the IICRC S520 assessment protocol: visual inspection, odour assessment (musty/earthy indicators), identification of moisture sources, and determination of the mould condition (1 through 3). Students learn to recognise hidden mould indicators — staining patterns, warped materials, persistent odour without visible growth — and when to recommend third-party environmental testing (air and surface sampling). The lesson covers the Australian regulatory context including WHS obligations, EPA waste disposal requirements, and the role of occupational hygienists. Scope decisions at this stage determine whether the project is a minor surface clean or a full containment remediation.

**Standards:** IICRC S520 Sections 5–8, Model WHS Act 2011

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will assess a simulated mould scenario from photos and data, determining the mould condition and recommending the assessment pathway.

---

### Lesson 4.2 — Air Sampling & Surface Testing (Video, 15 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Explain the purpose and limitations of air and surface sampling for mould
- Identify when sampling is required versus when visual assessment is sufficient
- Interpret laboratory results at a level sufficient for scoping decisions

**Key Concepts:** Environmental sampling supports scoping decisions when the extent of contamination is uncertain or when clearance testing is required after remediation. This lesson covers the two primary sampling methods: air sampling (spore trap cassettes measuring airborne spore counts) and surface sampling (tape lift, swab or bulk samples identifying species on materials). Students learn that sampling is not always necessary — IICRC S520 states that if visible mould is present and the moisture source is identified, remediation can proceed without laboratory confirmation. However, sampling becomes essential for hidden mould, post-remediation clearance, and situations involving occupant health concerns. The lesson covers how to read a laboratory report and use results to inform scope boundaries.

**Standards:** IICRC S520 Section 6 (Assessment and Investigation)

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will review a sample laboratory report and determine its implications for scope boundaries.

---

### Lesson 4.3 — Containment & Negative Air Requirements (Video, 12 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Design appropriate containment for mould remediation based on project scale
- Specify negative air pressure requirements and HEPA filtration
- Include containment setup and monitoring in the scope of works

**Key Concepts:** Containment prevents cross-contamination during mould remediation and is a critical scope component. This lesson covers containment levels: minimal (Condition 1 — cleaning only), limited (Condition 2 — polyethylene barriers, single HEPA AFD), and full (Condition 3 — double-barrier polyethylene, negative air pressure, decontamination chamber). Students learn to specify the required components in a scope: polyethylene sheeting (6 mil minimum), HEPA-filtered air filtration devices (AFDs), negative pressure differential (typically -5 Pa), seal materials, and warning signage. The lesson addresses ongoing monitoring requirements — differential pressure monitoring, visual inspection of barrier integrity, and the scope implications of a containment breach.

**Standards:** IICRC S520 Section 10 (Remediation Procedures), AS 1668.2 (ventilation)

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will specify containment requirements for a Condition 2 mould remediation in a three-room residential scenario.

---

### Lesson 4.4 — Scope Writing: Mould Jobs (Text, 25 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Write a complete scope of works for a mould remediation project
- Include all required phases: assessment, containment, remediation, clearance and reinstatement
- Address regulatory requirements including waste disposal and WHS controls

**Key Concepts:** Mould remediation scopes follow a structured sequence that differs from water damage: assessment and source control, containment setup, removal of affected materials, HEPA vacuuming and treatment of remaining substrates, clearance testing, and reinstatement. This lesson takes students through each phase with line-item detail. Special attention is given to waste disposal (double-bagging, transport to licensed facility), worker PPE requirements (P2 respirators, disposable coveralls, gloves), and clearance criteria (visual inspection plus environmental sampling). Students practise writing scope notes that address the assessor's findings and demonstrate compliance with IICRC S520 and WHS requirements.

**Standards:** IICRC S520, Model WHS Act 2011, EPA waste guidelines (state-specific)

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will produce a complete mould remediation scope for a simulated Condition 2 residential scenario.

## Module 5: Fire & Smoke Scoping

### Lesson 5.1 — Fire Damage Categories (Video, 14 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Classify fire damage by type and severity (structure fire, content fire, protein fire, complex fire)
- Assess structural integrity concerns and identify demolition requirements
- Determine the scoping approach based on fire damage type

**Key Concepts:** Fire damage scoping requires understanding the different combustion residues and their impact on building materials and contents. This lesson covers four fire damage categories: structure fires (large-scale, often requiring demolition and rebuild), content fires (localised, focused on decontamination and restoration), protein fires (kitchen fires producing nearly invisible but highly odorous residues), and complex fires (involving hazardous materials, chemicals or synthetics). Students learn to assess structural integrity — charred timber depth, steel deflection, concrete spalling — and determine which elements require structural engineer assessment. The scoping approach varies dramatically by fire type, and the estimator must correctly classify the loss to produce an accurate scope.

**Standards:** IICRC S540 (referenced informally — not yet a full standard), Building Code of Australia (BCA)

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will classify three fire scenarios by type and identify the primary scoping priorities for each.

---

### Lesson 5.2 — Contents Inventory & Pack-Out Procedures (Video, 18 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Conduct a systematic contents inventory for a fire-damaged property
- Apply pack-out procedures including documentation, labelling and chain of custody
- Determine restorability of contents based on material type and exposure

**Key Concepts:** Contents form a significant portion of most fire damage claims. This lesson covers the contents inventory and pack-out process: room-by-room documentation with photographs, description and condition assessment for every item. Students learn to use contents inventory software and spreadsheet templates. The pack-out process follows a chain of custody — every item is tagged, photographed, and logged before boxing and transport to a cleaning facility or secure storage. Restorability depends on material type (hard goods vs soft goods), exposure to heat, smoke, soot and water, and the cost-effectiveness of restoration versus replacement. The lesson addresses common disputes: sentimental value vs actual cash value, and how to document the condition clearly to support the claim.

**Standards:** IICRC S540 (referenced), Insurance Council of Australia General Insurance Code of Practice

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will complete a contents inventory for a simulated lounge room with 15 items, classifying each as restorable, non-restorable or requiring specialist assessment.

---

### Lesson 5.3 — Smoke Odour Assessment (Video, 12 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Assess smoke and odour penetration across different building materials and systems
- Identify the appropriate deodorisation method based on residue type and substrate
- Include odour treatment in the scope with defensible line items

**Key Concepts:** Smoke odour is one of the most challenging aspects of fire damage scoping. This lesson covers how smoke residues penetrate porous materials (soft furnishings, insulation, carpet, timber) and HVAC systems, creating persistent odour that standard cleaning cannot resolve. Students learn the three main deodorisation approaches: thermal fogging (for protein and light smoke), hydroxyl generation (broad-spectrum, occupied-space safe), and ozone treatment (heavy smoke, unoccupied space only). Each method is matched to appropriate scenarios. The scope must specify the treatment method, duration, number of generators/foggers, and access requirements. The lesson highlights that odour assessment is subjective and recommends third-party verification for clearance on disputed claims.

**Standards:** IICRC S540 (referenced)

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will select the appropriate deodorisation method and write scope line items for two fire scenarios (protein fire vs structure fire).

---

### Lesson 5.4 — Scope Writing: Fire & Smoke Jobs (Text, 25 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Write a complete scope of works for fire and smoke damage restoration
- Structure the scope by phase: make-safe, demolition, decontamination, contents, reinstatement
- Address asbestos, WHS and structural assessment requirements in the scope

**Key Concepts:** Fire damage scopes are typically the most complex in restoration, involving multiple trades and extended timelines. This lesson guides students through the full scope structure: emergency make-safe (board-up, tarping, temporary power), demolition (structural assessment first, then controlled removal of damaged elements), decontamination (soot removal, antimicrobial treatment, HVAC cleaning), contents management (pack-out, restoration, storage), and reinstatement (rebuild to pre-loss condition). Students learn to manage the scope-to-price conversion for fire jobs, which often involve subcontracted trades (electricians, plumbers, plasterers, painters). Asbestos-containing materials are a critical consideration — any pre-1990 building requires ACM assessment before demolition begins. The lesson addresses coordination with building certifiers and the Building Code of Australia.

**Standards:** IICRC S540 (referenced), BCA, Model WHS Act 2011, state-based asbestos regulations

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will produce a phased fire damage scope for a simulated commercial kitchen fire.

## Module 6: Pricing & Estimating

### Lesson 6.1 — Industry Pricing Software (Xactimate, SimPRO) (Video, 22 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Navigate Xactimate to produce line-item estimates using Australian price lists
- Understand SimPRO's role in job management, quoting and invoicing
- Select the appropriate tool based on the client (insurer vs private)

**Key Concepts:** Digital pricing software is essential for modern restoration estimating. This lesson covers the two primary platforms used in Australia. Xactimate is the insurance industry standard — claims adjusters expect estimates in Xactimate format with Australian price lists. Students learn to navigate the interface, select line items (by category code), adjust quantities, and add custom line items with supporting notes. SimPRO is the job management platform favoured by restoration companies for operational efficiency — quoting, scheduling, purchase orders, invoicing and reporting in one system. Students understand when to use each: Xactimate for insurance claims, SimPRO for private work and internal management. The lesson covers common pitfalls including outdated price lists, incorrect unit of measure, and missing mobilisation charges.

**Standards:** Industry practice (Xactimate Australian price lists updated quarterly)

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will build a basic water damage estimate in Xactimate using provided scope data.

---

### Lesson 6.2 — Labour Costing & Overhead Recovery (Video, 18 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Calculate the fully loaded cost of a restoration technician including all on-costs
- Apply overhead recovery methodology to job-level pricing
- Avoid common labour costing errors that erode profitability

**Key Concepts:** Labour is the largest cost component in most restoration jobs. This lesson teaches students to calculate the "fully loaded" rate for a technician: base hourly rate + superannuation (currently 11.5%) + workers' compensation premium + annual leave loading + payroll tax (if applicable) + training and certification costs. Students then learn overhead recovery — allocating a share of the company's fixed costs (rent, insurance, vehicles, administration, software licences) to each job. Two methods are covered: percentage-based recovery (applying a fixed overhead percentage to direct costs) and hourly overhead rate (dividing total annual overhead by productive labour hours). The lesson illustrates how under-recovering overhead leads to unprofitable jobs even when labour rates appear competitive.

**Standards:** Fair Work Australia rates, SBR (Single Touch Payroll), ATO superannuation obligations

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will calculate the fully loaded hourly rate for a technician using a provided cost template.

---

### Lesson 6.3 — Equipment Hire vs. Own Rates (Video, 14 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Compare hire rates with ownership cost rates for common restoration equipment
- Apply appropriate equipment rates in estimates based on business model
- Calculate the break-even point for equipment purchase vs ongoing hire

**Key Concepts:** Restoration companies must decide whether to hire or own their equipment fleet, and this decision directly affects estimate pricing. This lesson covers the main equipment categories: dehumidifiers (refrigerant and desiccant), air movers, HEPA air filtration devices, moisture meters, thermal imaging cameras, and generators. For each category, students compare typical Australian hire rates (daily and weekly) against ownership costs (purchase price, depreciation, maintenance, calibration, storage, transport). The break-even analysis determines the utilisation rate at which owning becomes more economical than hiring. Students learn to apply the correct rate in estimates — hire rates for hired equipment, internal charge-out rates for owned equipment — and how to present equipment costs transparently to insurers.

**Standards:** ATO depreciation schedules, industry hire rate benchmarks

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will complete a break-even analysis for a refrigerant dehumidifier using provided hire and purchase data.

---

### Lesson 6.4 — Margin, Markup & Profitability (Video, 16 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Distinguish between gross margin and markup and apply each correctly
- Set pricing to achieve target profitability on restoration jobs
- Identify common margin leaks and how to prevent them

**Key Concepts:** Profitability in restoration requires a clear understanding of the relationship between costs, markup and margin. This lesson clarifies the critical distinction: markup is calculated on cost (e.g., 43% markup on $10,000 cost = $14,300 selling price), while gross margin is calculated on selling price (e.g., 30% margin means $10,000 cost requires a $14,286 selling price). Students learn to set target margins appropriate for the Australian restoration market, accounting for competition, insurer expectations and business maturity. The lesson identifies common margin leaks: unbilled travel time, under-counted consumables, unrecovered equipment mobilisation/demobilisation, and scope creep without corresponding supplements. Students practise converting between markup and margin to ensure pricing accuracy.

**Standards:** Industry benchmarks (ARCBA, Restoration Industry Association guidelines)

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will price a provided scope to achieve a 30% gross margin and identify three potential margin leaks.

## Module 7: Insurance & Client Communication

### Lesson 7.1 — Working with Loss Adjusters (Video, 20 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Describe the loss adjuster's role, responsibilities and authority in the claims process
- Communicate effectively with loss adjusters to achieve fair scope approval
- Manage common friction points including scope reductions and delays

**Key Concepts:** Loss adjusters are the insurer's appointed representatives who investigate claims, assess the loss, and recommend settlements. This lesson clarifies their role — they are not the restorer's adversary, but they are incentivised to ensure claims are reasonable and supported. Students learn to work professionally with loss adjusters: presenting scopes in a clear, evidence-based format; responding to information requests promptly; and managing disagreements through the supplement process rather than confrontation. The lesson covers the different types of adjusters (staff adjusters employed by the insurer vs independent adjusters contracted on a per-claim basis) and how this affects their authority and responsiveness. Students learn the importance of building long-term relationships based on trust, accuracy and professionalism.

**Standards:** Insurance Contracts Act 1984 (Cth), General Insurance Code of Practice

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will role-play a scope presentation to a loss adjuster using a prepared scope document.

---

### Lesson 7.2 — Supplement Writing & Negotiation (Video, 18 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Identify when a supplement is required during a restoration project
- Write a compelling supplement supported by evidence and standard references
- Negotiate supplement approval without damaging the adjuster relationship

**Key Concepts:** Supplements are a normal part of restoration — hidden damage is common and initial scopes cannot account for every contingency. This lesson teaches the professional approach to supplements: document the additional damage immediately upon discovery (photos, readings, measurements), stop work in the affected area, notify the insurer/adjuster in writing, and submit a formal supplement request with line-item detail and supporting evidence. Students learn the "supplement triangle" — the three elements adjusters need to approve additional work: evidence of the additional damage, justification for why it was not foreseeable, and standard reference for the proposed remediation approach. The lesson covers negotiation techniques that maintain the restorer's professional standing while advocating for fair compensation.

**Standards:** IICRC S500, Insurance Contracts Act 1984 (Cth)

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will write a supplement for a simulated scenario where hidden mould is discovered behind a wall during a water damage restoration.

---

### Lesson 7.3 — Client Expectations & Dispute Resolution (Video, 15 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Set and manage client expectations throughout the restoration timeline
- Handle client complaints and disputes professionally and constructively
- Understand the dispute resolution pathways available in Australia

**Key Concepts:** Managing client expectations is as important as the technical scope. This lesson covers the communication framework for client management: setting realistic expectations at the first meeting (timeline, disruption, noise), providing regular progress updates (weekly minimum), documenting all decisions and approvals in writing, and handling the emotional dimension of property loss. Students learn the dispute resolution hierarchy in Australia: internal complaint resolution first, then the insurer's Internal Dispute Resolution (IDR) process, followed by the Australian Financial Complaints Authority (AFCA) for unresolved insurance disputes. For private work, state-based consumer protection laws (Australian Consumer Law) and fair trading bodies provide the resolution pathway. The lesson emphasises that most disputes arise from communication failures, not quality failures.

**Standards:** Australian Consumer Law, AFCA, Insurance Contracts Act 1984 (Cth)

**Practical Takeaway:** Students will draft a client communication plan template covering five key touchpoints across a restoration project lifecycle.

## Module 8: Capstone — Full Job Scope

### Lesson 8.1 — Case Study: Residential Water Loss (Text, 30 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Apply all scoping skills to a complex residential water damage scenario
- Produce a complete scope from initial inspection data through to priced estimate
- Demonstrate integration of standards references, evidence documentation and pricing

**Key Concepts:** This capstone case study presents a realistic residential water loss: a burst flexi-hose in a first-floor bathroom of a two-storey brick veneer home, affecting the bathroom, hallway, two bedrooms (upper level), and the living area and kitchen ceiling (lower level). Category 1 water, Class 3 damage. Students receive the full data package: floor plans, moisture map readings, photographs, materials list, and the homeowner's verbal account. Working through the scenario end to end, students produce: a site inspection report, moisture map, affected materials inventory, drying equipment plan, complete scope of works with line items for each trade, and a priced estimate targeting 30% gross margin. The case study includes a complication — suspected ACMs in the bathroom floor tiles — requiring students to address the WHS and asbestos assessment requirements in their scope.

**Standards:** IICRC S500, AS/NZS 4349.1, Model WHS Act 2011, state asbestos regulations

**Practical Takeaway:** Students submit a complete scope package (inspection report + moisture map + scope + estimate) for peer and instructor review.

---

### Lesson 8.2 — Case Study: Commercial Fire Damage (Text, 30 min)

**Learning Objectives:**

- Apply all scoping skills to a commercial fire damage scenario with multiple trades
- Manage the additional complexity of commercial properties (lease obligations, business interruption, compliance)
- Produce a phased scope addressing make-safe, decontamination, contents and reinstatement

**Key Concepts:** This case study presents a commercial fire scenario: a kitchen fire in a restaurant tenancy within a mixed-use building, with smoke damage extending to two adjacent retail tenancies and the common area. Structure fire with significant protein residue, soot deposition and water damage from fire suppression. Students must navigate the additional complexity of commercial properties: multiple stakeholders (building owner, tenant, insurer, body corporate), lease obligations (who is responsible for what), business interruption considerations (timeline pressure), and compliance requirements (food safety, fire services certification, BCA). The scope must be phased — make-safe (board-up, temporary hoarding), demolition and strip-out, decontamination (soot, odour, HVAC), contents pack-out and restoration, and staged reinstatement with trade coordination. Students also address the smoke migration to adjacent tenancies.

**Standards:** IICRC S540 (referenced), BCA, relevant state food safety legislation, Model WHS Act 2011

**Practical Takeaway:** Students submit a phased commercial fire scope with a trade coordination schedule for instructor review.
